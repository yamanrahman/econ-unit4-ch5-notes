4.3.5a Public Expenditure — Types & Determinants ▼

Three Types of Government Spending

Capital expenditure: Spending on long-lived assets that increase the productive capacity of the economy — roads, hospitals, schools, broadband infrastructure. Creates assets that provide services over many years. Also called public investment.
Current expenditure: Spending on the day-to-day running of public services — wages of public sector workers (teachers, nurses, soldiers), consumables, maintenance. Does not create lasting assets. The largest component of government spending in most economies.
Transfer payments: Government payments to individuals that are not in exchange for goods or services — state pension, unemployment benefit (Jobseeker's Allowance), Universal Credit, tax credits, disability benefits. Redistributes income but is not counted in GDP (no value added).

Determinants of Government Spending

1. Demographic factors

Ageing population increases age-related spending (state pension, NHS, social care). UK: state pension is the single largest benefit — over £100bn annually. As the dependency ratio rises, current expenditure and transfer payments grow as a share of GDP even without policy changes.

2. Economic cycle

Government spending automatically rises during recessions (automatic stabilisers: rising unemployment benefit) and falls during booms. Cyclical government spending helps stabilise aggregate demand — Keynesian automatic stabilisers reduce multiplier effects of output gaps.

3. Political choices

Governments choose the composition of spending based on political priorities — public goods provision, redistribution levels, infrastructure investment. These choices reflect ideological positions (Keynesian demand management vs supply-side fiscal conservatism).

4. National debt service costs

High national debt increases interest payments — crowding out other government spending. UK interest payments exceeded £100bn in 2022–23 following quantitative easing normalisation. As debt rises, a larger share of tax revenue goes to debt service, constraining other expenditure.

Wagner's Law

Wagner's Law (1883): As economies develop and real incomes rise, government spending tends to grow as a share of GDP. Three reasons: (1) rising demand for public goods (law and order, education) with income; (2) economies of scale in public provision; (3) natural monopoly features of infrastructure require public provision. Adolph Wagner predicted this secular trend of "expanding state activity."
Wagner's Law is excellent context for any essay on government spending trends. It explains why government spending as a % of GDP tends to rise over time in all developed economies — not just due to policy choices but due to structural demand-side and supply-side forces. Evaluate: causality may run both ways; some argue rising spending reflects political capture rather than genuine demand.
4.3.5b Taxation — Types, Objectives & the Laffer Curve ▼

Direct vs Indirect Taxation

Direct tax: Levied directly on income, wealth, or profits of individuals and firms. The taxpayer pays the tax directly to the government. Examples: income tax, corporation tax, capital gains tax, inheritance tax, National Insurance contributions.
Indirect tax: Levied on expenditure — on the purchase of goods and services. Can be passed on to consumers (incidence shifting). Examples: VAT (20% in UK), fuel duty, alcohol duty, tobacco duty, air passenger duty.

Progressive, Proportional, and Regressive Taxes

Progressive tax: Average tax rate rises with income — higher earners pay a larger percentage of their income in tax. UK income tax: 20% basic rate, 40% higher rate, 45% additional rate. Capital gains tax also progressive.
Proportional (flat) tax: Average tax rate is constant regardless of income — all pay the same percentage. National Insurance contributions approximate this in some bands. Proposed by supply-side economists as efficiency-enhancing.
Regressive tax: Average tax rate falls as income rises — lower earners pay a higher percentage of income in tax. VAT is regressive because low-income households spend a higher proportion of income on consumption. Similarly, fuel duty, council tax (on property rather than income).

Canons of Taxation (Adam Smith)

Adam Smith (1776) identified four principles of good taxation: (1) Equity — taxes should be fair and proportional to ability to pay; (2) Certainty — the amount, timing, and manner of payment should be clear; (3) Convenience — taxes should be easy to pay; (4) Economy — cost of collection should be low relative to revenue raised.

The Laffer Curve

Laffer Curve (Arthur Laffer, 1974): At a tax rate of 0%, tax revenue = 0. At a tax rate of 100%, tax revenue = 0 (nobody works). Between these extremes, revenue first rises then falls as rates increase. There exists a revenue-maximising tax rate (t*) beyond which higher rates reduce total revenue.
Tax Revenue = Tax Rate × Tax Base — as rate rises beyond t*, the tax base shrinks faster than the rate rises, so revenue falls

Mechanisms via which high rates reduce the tax base

  • Supply-side effects: High marginal rates reduce incentive to work, invest, or take entrepreneurial risk — reducing taxable income
  • Avoidance and evasion: Higher rates increase incentive to use offshore accounts, complex corporate structures, or cash-in-hand transactions
  • Capital and talent flight: High-earners and firms relocate to lower-tax jurisdictions

Evaluation of the Laffer Curve

  • The position of t* is unknown and contested — empirical estimates for the UK suggest it may be between 50–70%. At the UK top rate of 45%, most economists believe we are on the upward-sloping part of the curve (raising the top rate would raise revenue).
  • The Laffer Curve was used to justify Reagan's supply-side tax cuts (1981) — but evidence suggests these primarily benefited high earners without significantly expanding the tax base
  • Diamond & Saez (2011) estimate the revenue-maximising top marginal rate is approximately 70% for the USA — well above current rates
Examiners love the Laffer Curve but want evaluative depth. Key point: "The Laffer Curve is theoretically uncontroversial — very high rates must reduce revenue. The empirical question is where t* lies. Diamond and Saez (2011) estimate ~70% for the USA, suggesting most developed economies are on the upward slope and raising top rates would increase revenue. The curve is often misused to justify all tax cuts regardless of starting rate."
4.3.5c Fiscal Deficit, National Debt: Structural vs Cyclical ▼

The Fiscal Deficit

Fiscal (budget) deficit: When government spending (G) exceeds tax revenue (T) in a given period. Deficit = G − T > 0. A deficit must be financed by borrowing — the government issues gilts (bonds) to the private sector and foreign investors.
National (public) debt: The accumulated stock of outstanding government borrowing. It rises by the amount of each annual deficit (and falls with a surplus). The national debt is a stock; the deficit is a flow.
National Debt(t) = National Debt(t-1) + Fiscal Deficit(t) — debt accumulates from deficits and falls with surpluses

Structural vs Cyclical Deficit

Cyclical deficit: The component of the deficit that arises automatically due to the economic cycle. In a recession, tax revenues fall (lower incomes, profits, spending) and transfer payments rise (more unemployment benefit). This component disappears when the economy returns to trend growth.
Structural deficit: The component of the deficit that would persist even if the economy were operating at its potential (trend) level of output. It reflects a fundamental imbalance between government spending commitments and tax revenues — a permanent mismatch requiring policy action to correct.

Debt-to-GDP Ratio

The fiscal sustainability of government debt is assessed by the debt-to-GDP ratio rather than the absolute level of debt. A country's capacity to service debt depends on the size of its economy relative to the debt burden. UK debt-to-GDP has risen from ~40% pre-2008 to over 100% by 2023.

Debt sustainability condition: (r − g) × (D/Y) < Primary Surplus/Y — where r = interest rate, g = growth rate, D = debt, Y = GDP

If r > g (interest rate exceeds growth rate), debt grows as a share of GDP even with no primary deficit — reinforcing the concern about Piketty's r > g analysis applied to government debt dynamics.

Crowding Out

Crowding out: The argument that government borrowing reduces private investment by (1) competing for loanable funds, pushing up real interest rates, and (2) reducing business confidence. If government borrowing fully crowds out an equal amount of private investment, fiscal stimulus has zero net effect on aggregate demand (full crowding out).

Evaluation: Crowding out is most likely when the economy is operating at full capacity (classical range of AS). In a recession with a large output gap and near-zero interest rates, crowding out is minimal — the government borrows funds that would otherwise be idle (Keynesian liquidity trap logic). The degree of crowding out is therefore state-dependent.

The crowding out debate is the central conflict between Keynesian and classical fiscal policy views. Key argument: "Crowding out is most significant when the economy is at full employment — additional borrowing competes for existing savings. During recessions, when the economy has spare capacity and interest rates are low, crowding out is minimal because government borrows from idle savings. The 2009 fiscal stimulus had large multiplier effects precisely because crowding out was limited at the zero lower bound."
4.3.5d Fiscal Policy — Expansionary, Contractionary & Multiplier ▼

Types of Fiscal Policy

Expansionary fiscal policy: Increasing government spending (G) and/or reducing taxes (T) to boost aggregate demand. Results in a larger fiscal deficit. Used to close a negative output gap (recession). Associated with Keynesian demand management.
Contractionary fiscal policy (austerity): Reducing government spending and/or raising taxes to reduce aggregate demand or improve fiscal sustainability. Reduces the deficit. Can cause recession if implemented during a downturn — risk of self-defeating austerity.

The Fiscal Multiplier

Fiscal multiplier: The ratio of the change in real GDP to the change in government spending (or taxes) that caused it. A multiplier > 1 means government spending increases output by more than the initial spending — via the Keynesian income-expenditure mechanism.
Multiplier (k) = 1 / (1 − MPC) = 1 / MPS — where MPC = marginal propensity to consume, MPS = marginal propensity to save

If MPC = 0.8, k = 1/(1-0.8) = 5. Each £1 of government spending generates £5 of GDP. In practice, the multiplier is much lower because some income leaks to: taxation (t), imports (m), and saving (s).

Realistic multiplier = 1 / (1 − MPC(1−t) + m) — where t = marginal tax rate, m = marginal propensity to import

Factors affecting the size of the multiplier

  • MPC: Higher MPC → larger multiplier. The poor have a higher MPC → redistribution to low-income households has a larger multiplier effect than tax cuts for the wealthy.
  • Openness to trade: High import propensity (open economies) → more leakage → smaller multiplier. UK's multiplier is lower than the USA's due to higher trade openness.
  • Spare capacity: If the economy has a large output gap, the multiplier is higher (additional spending generates real output). At full employment, spending is purely inflationary — multiplier approaches zero in real terms.
  • Crowding out: If government borrowing raises interest rates and crowds out private investment, the net multiplier is reduced.
  • Ricardian equivalence (Barro, 1974): If households expect higher future taxes to finance current deficits, they save more now to pay future taxes — completely offsetting the demand stimulus. Multiplier = 0. Empirically rejected at moderate debt levels but relevant at very high debt.

Automatic Stabilisers vs Discretionary Fiscal Policy

Automatic stabilisers: Built-in fiscal mechanisms that automatically reduce the amplitude of economic cycles without requiring policy action. In recession: tax revenues fall, transfer payments rise → deficit increases → supports AD. In boom: tax revenues rise, transfers fall → surplus increases → dampens AD.

Discretionary fiscal policy is deliberate changes to G or T. Requires: (1) recognition lag (identifying the shock), (2) decision lag (parliamentary process), (3) implementation lag (spending programmes take time to deploy). These lags mean discretionary fiscal policy can destabilise the cycle if poorly timed.

4.3.5e The 2008 Financial Crisis — Demand-Side Policy Response ▼

Causes of the 2008 Financial Crisis

The 2008 global financial crisis originated in the US sub-prime mortgage market. Key mechanisms:

  • Sub-prime lending: US banks lent to high-risk borrowers at low "teaser" rates, then rising interest rates caused mass default
  • Securitisation and CDOs: Sub-prime mortgages were packaged into Collateralised Debt Obligations (CDOs) and sold globally — spreading toxic assets through the international financial system
  • Credit rating failure: Rating agencies (Moody's, S&P) gave AAA ratings to complex CDOs, misrepresenting default risk
  • Leverage: Investment banks borrowed heavily to purchase these securities — when prices fell, losses exceeded capital buffers, triggering bank failures (Lehman Brothers, September 2008)
  • Contagion: Interbank lending froze as banks couldn't assess counterparty risk — credit crunch spread globally

Government Policy Responses

1. Bank Bailouts

Governments recapitalised failing banks to prevent systemic collapse. UK: £500bn banking package (October 2008) — nationalisation of RBS and Lloyds. Rationale: banks are systemically important; their collapse would destroy the payments system and credit supply, causing depression. Criticism: moral hazard — "too big to fail" increases future risk-taking.

2. Expansionary Fiscal Policy

G20 countries implemented coordinated fiscal stimulus in 2009. UK: VAT cut from 17.5% to 15% (temporary boost to consumer spending), increased capital spending. IMF estimated the global fiscal stimulus worth ~2% of world GDP. Outcome: avoided a second Great Depression; GDP fell ~5% but recovered faster than in the 1930s.

3. Ultra-Low Interest Rates

Bank of England cut base rate to 0.5% by March 2009 (then 0.1% in 2020). Reduces borrowing costs, stimulates consumption and investment. Limitation: at the zero lower bound (ZLB), conventional monetary policy is ineffective — the liquidity trap (Keynes).

4. Quantitative Easing (QE)

Quantitative Easing: The central bank creates new money electronically and uses it to purchase assets (primarily government bonds) from the private sector. This increases bank reserves, reduces long-term interest rates (via portfolio rebalancing), and aims to stimulate lending and asset prices. Bank of England QE: £895bn by 2021.

Transmission mechanism: QE → lower long-term bond yields → higher asset prices → wealth effect → higher consumption + investment. Criticism: QE disproportionately benefits asset owners (wealthy), worsening inequality. Limited pass-through to credit availability when banks are rebuilding capital.

5. Post-Crisis Austerity (2010–2019)

UK Coalition government implemented fiscal consolidation: spending cuts and tax rises to reduce the structural deficit. IMF later admitted multipliers were larger than assumed (1.5–2 rather than 0.5), meaning austerity reduced output more than expected. The self-defeating austerity debate: if multipliers are large, austerity reduces GDP → reduces tax revenues → deficit may not fall as projected.

The 2008 crisis policy response is a treasure trove of exam material. Key chain: financial crisis → demand collapse → ZLB → Keynesian fiscal stimulus → multiplier → recovery. Then: austerity debate — if multiplier > 1, austerity is self-defeating. IMF (Blanchard & Leigh, 2013) found multipliers during austerity were 1.5–2, vindicating Keynesian criticism of austerity.
4.3.5f TNC Regulation — Transfer Pricing & Tax Avoidance ▼

TNC Tax Avoidance Strategies

TNCs operate across multiple tax jurisdictions, creating opportunities to minimise global tax liabilities through legal (avoidance) and illegal (evasion) strategies.

Transfer Pricing

Transfer pricing: TNCs set prices for transactions between their own subsidiaries in different countries. By manipulating these internal prices, they can shift profits from high-tax jurisdictions to low-tax jurisdictions. For example: a subsidiary in a low-tax country charges a high price for a patent or service to a subsidiary in a high-tax country — reducing profits in the high-tax country and raising them in the low-tax country.

The OECD estimates that profit shifting by multinationals costs governments $100–240bn annually in lost corporate tax revenue — approximately 4–10% of global corporate tax revenues. Developing countries are proportionally most affected because they rely more heavily on corporate tax.

Debt Shifting (Thin Capitalisation)

TNCs structure their subsidiaries in high-tax countries with high internal debt from subsidiaries in low-tax countries. Interest payments reduce taxable profits in the high-tax country, shifting profits to the low-tax jurisdiction where interest income is taxed at a lower rate.

Treaty Shopping

TNCs route transactions through countries with favourable bilateral tax treaties to minimise withholding taxes on dividends, royalties, and interest. Ireland, Luxembourg, and the Netherlands have been used extensively for this purpose.

Regulation Attempts

OECD Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS)

BEPS (2015): OECD/G20 initiative to reduce corporate tax avoidance by TNCs. Key measures: (1) Country-by-country reporting — TNCs must report profits, taxes, and employees in each jurisdiction; (2) Action against aggressive transfer pricing; (3) Hybrid mismatch rules to prevent "stateless income"; (4) Substance requirements — tax benefits only where genuine economic activity occurs.

Global Minimum Corporate Tax (Pillar Two, 2021)

G7/OECD agreement: a global minimum corporate tax rate of 15% on the profits of large multinationals (revenue >€750m), effective from 2024. Countries below 15% must impose a "top-up tax" to bring the effective rate to 15%. Aims to end the race to the bottom on corporate tax rates. Critics: 15% is too low — many argued for 21%+. Tax havens like Cayman Islands outside the agreement.

Evaluation of TNC Regulation

✓ Case for regulation
  • Revenue loss from avoidance reduces public spending capacity
  • Unfair competitive advantage vs domestic firms (who pay full rate)
  • Erodes public trust in the tax system
  • BEPS has increased transparency and reduced some avoidance
  • Global minimum rate ends race to bottom on corporate rates
✗ Limits of regulation
  • Transfer pricing: very difficult to assess "arm's length" price for intangibles (patents, brands)
  • Regulatory arbitrage: TNCs shift to new unaddressed structures
  • Tax competition remains — countries attract FDI with low rates
  • 15% minimum may still incentivise profit-shifting below current rates
  • Developing countries excluded from OECD framework design
Excellent closing evaluation: "The fundamental challenge of TNC regulation is that it requires international coordination, but each country individually has an incentive to defect and attract capital with lower rates. The global minimum tax reduces but does not eliminate this — 15% is a floor, not a convergence point, and many tax haven jurisdictions remain outside the agreement."

4 marksDistinguish between the fiscal deficit and the national debt.

Model Answer

The fiscal deficit is a flow: the shortfall in a given period (typically a financial year) where government spending exceeds tax revenues (G − T > 0). It must be financed by borrowing — issuing government bonds.

The national debt is a stock: the total accumulated borrowing outstanding at any point in time. It rises by the amount of each year's deficit and falls with any surplus. The two are related: if the government runs a deficit every year, the national debt grows continuously. A government could run deficits for decades while the debt-to-GDP ratio still falls if GDP growth outpaces the deficit.

Mark scheme: 1 mark each: deficit as flow / debt as stock. 1 mark: deficit adds to debt each year. 1 mark: debt can fall as % of GDP even with deficit if growth exceeds deficit.
8 marksExplain the difference between a structural and a cyclical fiscal deficit and why this distinction matters for policy.

Model Answer

A cyclical deficit arises automatically during a recession: falling incomes reduce income tax and VAT receipts, rising unemployment increases benefit payments, and corporation tax falls with profits. This deficit disappears when the economy recovers — it is a symptom of the output gap, not a structural problem.

A structural deficit is the deficit that would persist even if the economy were operating at full capacity (the trend level of output). It reflects a permanent mismatch between government spending commitments and tax revenues at normal economic conditions — it will not automatically disappear with recovery.

The distinction matters profoundly for policy. A cyclical deficit should generally be tolerated or encouraged — it reflects automatic stabilisers reducing the severity of the downturn (Keynesian logic). Attempting to close a cyclical deficit during a recession through spending cuts (austerity) removes demand exactly when it is most needed, potentially deepening the recession.

A structural deficit, by contrast, requires deliberate fiscal consolidation — either permanent spending cuts or tax increases — because it will persist even after full recovery. The risk of confusing the two: governments that mistake a cyclical deficit for a structural one impose harmful austerity; governments that mistake a structural deficit for a cyclical one delay necessary adjustment, allowing debt to accumulate.

Band 4: Clear definitions of both concepts. Explanation of why cyclical deficit is self-correcting. Analysis of why structural deficit requires active policy. Evaluation: difficulty of measuring the output gap in real time makes the distinction hard to implement (OBR estimates subject to wide uncertainty bands).

Essay Plan 2 of 10
"Raising the top rate of income tax will always reduce tax revenues." Evaluate this view.
Define
Top rate of income tax; tax revenues = rate × tax base. Laffer Curve: at 0% and 100%, revenue = 0; revenue-maximising rate (t*) exists between these extremes.
Argument for
Laffer Curve logic: if rate is above t*, raising it further reduces labour supply, increases avoidance/evasion, and drives capital/talent flight → tax base shrinks more than rate rises → revenue falls. Supply-side economists (Reagan, Thatcher) used this to justify top rate cuts in 1980s.
Counter 1
Diamond & Saez (2011): revenue-maximising top rate estimated at ~70% for USA. UK top rate currently 45% — well below t*. Raising top rate from 45% to 50% likely raises revenue. The claim assumes we are above t*, which is empirically unsupported.
Counter 2
Elasticity of taxable income (ETI) is modest at low to moderate rates. Evidence from UK 50% rate (2010–2013): limited evidence of significant behavioural response; OBR estimated modest revenue gain.
Limit of counter
Long-run behavioural responses larger than short-run: investment in tax avoidance structures takes time. HMRC data show increased dividend extraction before top rate cut in 2013 — timing effects distort short-run evidence.
Conclude
"Always" is too strong. Whether raising the top rate reduces revenue depends on whether the current rate is above or below t*, the elasticity of taxable income, and the effectiveness of anti-avoidance legislation. At typical OECD top rates (40–50%), most evidence suggests raising the top rate increases revenue.

Essay Plan 3 of 10
Evaluate the impact of austerity on macroeconomic performance in the UK after the 2008 financial crisis.
Define
Austerity: fiscal consolidation — spending cuts and/or tax rises to reduce the structural deficit and stabilise the debt-to-GDP ratio. Context: UK deficit reached ~10% of GDP in 2009–10.
Argument for
Without consolidation, rising debt service costs crowd out public spending; potential sovereign debt crisis (cf. Greece 2010). Structural balance restores fiscal space for future downturns. Confidence channel: reducing debt uncertainty may support investment.
Argument against 1
IMF (Blanchard & Leigh, 2013): multipliers during austerity were 1.5–2, not 0.5 as assumed. Each £1 of spending cuts reduced GDP by £1.50–2. Austerity was self-defeating: it reduced GDP, reducing tax revenues, partially offsetting deficit reduction.
Argument against 2
UK productivity stagnated after 2008 — "productivity puzzle." Reduced public capital investment (schools, infrastructure) likely contributed to supply-side damage. Short-run austerity may have caused long-run hysteresis: unemployed workers lose skills, reducing potential output permanently.
Argument against 3
Inequality impact: welfare cuts hit poorest hardest. IFS: real incomes of lowest quintile fell most sharply during 2010–2018 austerity period. Social costs of reduced public services (NHS waiting times, homelessness) not captured in GDP.
Conclude
UK austerity reduced the deficit but at significant macroeconomic cost. At the ZLB with large multipliers, earlier fiscal consolidation was premature. A more gradual approach — consolidating once the economy had recovered (structural rather than cyclical deficit only) — would likely have delivered better outcomes for both growth and fiscal sustainability.

Essay Plan 4 of 10
Evaluate the view that indirect taxes are preferable to direct taxes as a way of raising government revenue.
Define
Direct tax: levied on income/profits directly (income tax, corporation tax). Indirect tax: levied on expenditure (VAT, excise duties). Evaluate on: efficiency, equity, revenue stability, avoidance potential.
For indirect
Lower administration cost — collected at point of sale. Less scope for avoidance — harder to hide consumption than income. Pigouvian: excise duties on harmful goods (tobacco, alcohol, fuel) correct negative externalities, raising revenue while improving efficiency.
Against indirect
Regressive: VAT takes higher % of income from poor than rich (all spend similar amounts but poor have lower incomes). Conflicts with equity objective. Violates Smith's canon of equity — those least able to pay bear proportionally higher burden.
For direct
Progressive — rises with ability to pay. Better for redistribution and reducing inequality. Stable revenue base in booms when incomes are high. Individualised — can target those most able to contribute.
Against direct
High avoidance scope — offshore accounts, corporate structures. May reduce work incentives at high rates (Laffer). Bracket creep: inflation pushes taxpayers into higher bands without real income increases.
Conclude
No single tax type is uniformly superior. Optimal tax design uses both: progressive direct taxes for redistribution and stability, indirect taxes (with exemptions for essentials) for efficiency and externality correction. The optimal mix depends on the government's relative priorities for equity, efficiency, and revenue stability.

Essay Plan 7 of 10
Evaluate the view that governments should always balance their budgets.
Define
Balanced budget: G = T every year. Distinguish from cyclically balanced (balanced over the full cycle) and structural balance (balanced at full employment).
Argument for
Prevents debt accumulation: r > g means debt-to-GDP ratio rises without primary surplus → eventual fiscal crisis. Maintains credibility with financial markets → lower borrowing costs. Inter-generational equity: current generation should not impose debt burden on future generations (Ricardo).
Against 1
Keynesian automatic stabilisers require running deficits in recessions: balancing budget annually forces pro-cyclical policy — cuts spending precisely when multiplier is highest and damage is greatest. This deepens recessions (1930s austerity → Great Depression).
Against 2
Capital expenditure argument (Golden Rule): borrowing for productive public investment (infrastructure, education) that raises future productivity is self-financing — raises future tax revenues to service the debt. Annual balance treats capital and current spending identically, discouraging productive investment.
Against 3
Low interest rates and r < g: when borrowing costs fall below growth rates, debt-to-GDP ratio falls even with primary deficits. Blanchard (2019): in "low r" environment, government debt is less costly than traditionally assumed.
Conclude
Strict annual balance is too rigid: forces pro-cyclical policy and prevents productive investment. More appropriate rules: structural balance (balance over the economic cycle), Golden Rule (borrow only for investment), and debt-to-GDP ceiling. Fiscal rules should be counter-cyclical, not pro-cyclical.

Evaluate the effectiveness of government intervention in regulating TNC tax avoidance.
Define
TNC tax avoidance: legal strategies to minimise tax (transfer pricing, debt shifting, treaty shopping). Costs: OECD estimates $100–240bn/year global revenue loss (4–10% of corporate tax). Regulation: BEPS, global minimum 15%, CbCR.
Case for BEPS
Country-by-country reporting increases transparency. Arm's length pricing rules reduce transfer pricing manipulation. Hybrid mismatch rules eliminate "stateless income" structures. 137 countries signed BEPS framework — broad participation.
Limit 1
Transfer pricing of intangibles (patents, algorithms, brands): no objective market price → TNCs retain flexibility to shift profits. Regulatory arbitrage: TNCs develop new structures faster than BEPS rules are updated.
Limit 2
15% global minimum: stops race to bottom but floor is low. Countries below 15% (Cayman, BVI) not OECD members → non-participants continue offering zero-tax environments. Large TNCs below revenue threshold excluded.
Limit 3
Enforcement asymmetry: developing countries lack technical capacity to audit complex TNC structures. Proportionally bear largest burden of revenue loss (corporate tax is larger share of their revenue) but have least enforcement capacity.
Conclude
BEPS and the global minimum rate represent genuine progress but remain insufficient. More ambitious reform (formulary apportionment — taxing profits where sales/employees are located rather than where profits are booked) would be more effective but requires deeper sovereignty concessions than most governments accept.

Essay Plan 9 of 10
Evaluate the role of fiscal policy in promoting economic growth in a developing economy.
Define
Fiscal policy: G and T decisions. Growth: sustainable increase in real GDP per capita. Developing economy context: low initial human capital, infrastructure deficit, weak institutions.
Channel 1: Public investment
Capital expenditure in roads, ports, power, broadband → reduces transactions costs → enables trade and specialisation → ↑TFP. Infrastructure bottlenecks are key constraint in many LDCs. WB: 1% ↑ infrastructure investment → ~1.5% ↑ GDP long-run.
Channel 2: Human capital
Public education and health spending → ↑productivity → ↑long-run growth. Conditional on quality of spending: teacher quality, access for girls (closing gender gap in education raises growth disproportionately — World Bank).
Channel 3: Demand management
Less relevant in LDCs with undeveloped financial markets and limited fiscal space. Multiplier smaller in open, low-income economies with high import propensity and limited automatic stabilisers.
Constraints
Fiscal space: narrow tax base, high informality, weak enforcement → low tax revenue → limited spending capacity. Tax revenue ≈ 15% of GDP in many LDCs vs 35–45% in OECD. Must borrow internationally → external debt risk.
Conclude
Fiscal policy can promote growth through infrastructure and human capital investment, but is constrained by limited fiscal space. Most critical reform: broaden the tax base (reduce informality, improve enforcement) to finance public investment without unsustainable debt. The composition of spending matters more than its level.

Essay Plan 10 of 10
Evaluate the view that rising national debt is always harmful to an economy.
Define
National debt: accumulated stock of government borrowing. Debt-to-GDP ratio: the meaningful measure of sustainability. UK: rose from ~40% to >100% of GDP post-2008.
Harmful argument 1
Rising debt service costs crowd out public spending. Higher real interest rates (risk premium) when debt is high → investment ↓ → growth ↓. Reinhart & Rogoff (2010) controversially claimed debt >90% GDP associated with lower growth (though later disputed due to data errors).
Harmful argument 2
Intergenerational burden: future taxpayers must service today's debt. Ricardian households anticipate future tax rises, increasing savings today and reducing current AD.
Counter 1
Blanchard (2019): when r < g (interest rate below growth rate), debt-to-GDP ratio falls automatically — no fiscal tightening needed. In low interest rate era, debt is cheap and sustainable even at high levels.
Counter 2
Composition matters: if debt finances productive public investment (infrastructure, education), future GDP and tax revenues rise to service it — self-financing. Golden Rule rationale.
Counter 3
Debt owned domestically: if debt is held by domestic investors, interest payments are a transfer within the economy (redistribution from taxpayers to bondholders) not a net burden on national income. Mostly applies to Japan (95%+ domestically held debt at >200% GDP).
Conclude
"Always harmful" is too strong. Rising debt from productive investment in low-rate environments may be sustainable and growth-enhancing. Rising debt from current consumption (transfer payments, current expenditure) without corresponding productivity gains is more harmful. The key variable is r vs g and the composition of debt-financed spending.
